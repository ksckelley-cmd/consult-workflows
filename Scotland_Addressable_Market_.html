Scotland is the UK’s most active spinout region outside the Golden Triangle—and the pipeline is funded

Scotland has 248 active life sciences ventures as of Q2 2025, making it the most prolific spinout region in the UK outside London and the Southeast. Edinburgh ranks second nationally for new spinout firms (86), behind only Oxford (110). Glasgow and Strathclyde both rank in the UK top 20.

Three Scottish universities are among the top 20 UK academic institutions for spinout activity. The University of Edinburgh alone produced 71 spinout companies between 2011 and early 2025. Equity investment in UK university spinouts increased 38% in 2024 to £2.6 billion. Life sciences and pharmaceuticals are the strongest sectors for new spinout creation nationally.

Edinburgh ranks first in the UK outside the Golden Triangle for deep tech VC investment. Glasgow ranks third. This is an accessible, low-CAC pipeline with deal flow and measurable capital behind it.

The combination product category is likely understated. Many companies currently classified as drug-only or device-only will require combination product regulatory navigation as they move from discovery toward clinical development and commercial delivery. The forward-looking pipeline—companies that will need this capability within 2–5 years—is materially larger.

The immediately addressable market is ~149 ventures—the digital health/SaMD, MedTech/device, and confirmed combination product categories. The diagnostics/IVD segment (~25) is partially addressable. Drug-only ventures (~74) become addressable as they mature into combination products requiring device-interface regulatory strategy.

No identified competitor occupies this position. Glasgow-based device boutiques (e.g., MD Compliance) are regulatory execution specialists—they are not positioned to deliver integrated strategic advisory. Engineering consultancies in the Cambridge corridor (Team Consulting, Sagentia) design and build products but do not integrate regulatory strategy with capital formation and investor narrative. Pharma regulatory firms (PharmaLex, Arriello) hold deep drug-side expertise but no above-the-line advisory capability. They are complementary partners, not competitors.

Glasgow client signals: NebuFlow and ScreenIn3D

NebuFlow (Glasgow, late-seed-to-early-venture) has £10M+ in combined grants and equity, with next-generation nebulizers for respiratory drug delivery—a drug-delivery device at the drug-device interface. This is the precise profile of the ideal client.

ScreenIn3D (University of Strathclyde, late-seed-to-early-venture) has $2M+ in combined grants and equity, with a lab-on-a-chip platform for drug testing on patient-derived tumor models—a device-enabled drug development tool at the preclinical device interface.

Combination products are the growth vector—three forward indicators signal expanding demand

The confirmed combination product count (~12) understates the real opportunity. Drug-device combination clients represent the highest-value engagements in the practice—larger, more complex, and more capital-intensive than standard device regulatory work, with disproportionate need for above-the-line advisory integrating regulatory pathway clarity with investor-grade financial narrative. Three institutional pipelines signal materially larger demand within 2–5 years:

1. Medicines Manufacturing Innovation Centre (MMIC), Renfrewshire

The MMIC supports lab-to-commercial scale-up for medicines. Biologics and advanced therapies moving through this facility will increasingly encounter drug-device interface questions—delivery mechanism design, regulatory classification, CMC integration. Companies using this infrastructure are likely combination product candidates. This facility represents both a client pipeline and an institutional relationship worth cultivating.

2. Glasgow Precision Medicine—Queen Elizabeth University Hospital

The Clinical Innovation Zone and Precision Medicine Scotland Innovation Centre are conducting population-level pharmacogenomic trials. Therapeutics emerging from pharmacogenomic research frequently require patient-specific delivery mechanisms—precisely where drug-device combination products are born. This is a forward indicator on a 2–5 year horizon.

3. Software-interfacing drug delivery devices (Edinburgh)

The 65% digital/AI orientation of Edinburgh’s spinouts is directly relevant. FDA, EMA, and MHRA are all developing frameworks for software-driven combination products. Edinburgh’s digital health companies building on drug delivery adjacencies fall into this intersection. The firm’s dual capability in SaMD and combination products positions it uniquely for this emerging segment.

These combination product opportunities are not fully accessible with current capability alone. The recommended sequence: partner with a pharma regulatory firm now (60–90 days to capability), then build or acquire within 12–24 months. This is the most executable path given timeline constraints and the rarity of the pharma-side practitioner profile required.
